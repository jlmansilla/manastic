The Good, the Bad, and the Ugly: Why it pays to be kind

 
 

My favorite book, after Pride and Prejudice, of course, is Winners & Losers by Sydney J. Harris.* It contains about 50 differentiators between Winners and Losers. One such quip goes as follows:

A winner seeks for the goodness in a bad man, and works with that part of him;

a loser looks only for the badness in a good man, and therefore finds it hard to work with anyone.

Boiled down, one key to success is to give people the benefit of the doubt and respond with kindness and empathy regardless of the way other people are interacting in the situation. This principle, as it relates to business, was solidified in my mind with a couple of different experiences weve had with our customers both in our day jobs as well as with The Scout App.

Every Monday at work Im in charge of sending out a batch of emails to customers who have registered semi-recently, asking them to review our product in one of the marketplaces where its available. I send out the email from my personal email en masse through a special program called mail merge for Gmail, which means that everyones responses come straight back into my inbox.  About half of the responses I get are customers delighted with the product, thanking us for our hard work and promising to absolutely give us five stars in the marketplace. The other half, however, are less pleasant to read and mostly contain a colorful mixture of swear words, conveying the basic message that theyd rather not be contacted in the future. I normally do O.K. responding in a civil fashion to the first couple of gnarly ones that come in, but by the time Ive seen a dozen Ive had about enough of these incredibly rude people, and I wish that theyd just click the Unsubscribe link in the email, since thats what its there for after all. 

Soon after sending out the blast of review-requesting emails this morning, I received the following response.

please stop emailing me. I do not use the tool, I installed it to help a colleague.

Not colorful, but also not incredibly encouraging, and as it was early on in the morning, I began to dread what was yet to come. But then, just two minutes later, the same customer responded again, with this:

I hit un subscribe sorry I am in a terrible mood! I wish you guys all the best

This simple apology (consequently the only apology for rudeness our support team has ever seen) completely changed my attitude towards this customer. I wanted to help him, empathize with him, and improve his day like he had just improved mine. So instead of wanting to write him a snarky response about how he was free to use the unsubscribe link at his earliest convenience, I wanted to tell him that I hoped his day got better. By simply being kind, he had instantly won me over. As a customer, those providing you the good/services are a lot more likely to help you get what you want if you are kind to them, and treat them like human beings even if it looks like a faceless email robot.

As the business, its equally important to respond with kindness in order to turn potential detractors into advocates. Being indifferent to your consumers can be almost as dangerous as being rude, and can turn potential advocates and life-time customers into just passers-by, or even worse, detractors.

Kesler has a couple of brothers who are very entrepreneurial minded, like he is. This means that when they do family Google Hangouts, they talk about big ideas theyve had or interesting things that theyve read. When Kesler brought up something hed read on a popular blog recently, one of his brothers (well call him Chandler T., no C. Tanner) mentioned that he used to follow that blog pretty regularly, but hadnt recently because of negative experiences hed had with the blog authorlets call him Blogger Tim.

Early on in the C. Tanners reading of the blog, he noticed a typo on the homepage, and shot Blogger Tim a quick email about it. Next time he visited the blog, the typo had been fixed, but Blogger Tim didnt acknowledge or thank him for the help. While that didnt bother C. Tanner too much, a while later he saw a potential typo in the authors bio and shot him a note again. This time, he got a very short response informing him that that was how it was supposed to be, and not in fact a typo. After his interactions with the author, C. Tanner was less impressed and read the blog less and less frequently. In the big picture, losing one reader probably doesnt seem like a big deal, but when you consider that it would have taken probably another 30 seconds to improve these two interactions, you realize that taking the time just might be worth it. Blogger Tim is probably an awesome guy. Sheesh, most of us are probably really great, wonderful people, but if you dont communicate that in these micro-interactions, sometimes people dont have a reason to get to know you better. And they most likely stop reading your blog and patronizing your popsicle stand.

 
 
 

On a positive note, you can turn upset customers into happy ones that want to help you improve your product if you respond with empathy and understanding. Weve been lucky enough to have a chance to put this into practice recently when one of our customers wrote in saying he felt like our pricing was too high and complicated and that hed been ripped off. Because Im still working on maturing so that I can deal with unhappy customers, Kesler was kind enough to take care of that one. Its a little bit long, but if you read it, you might learn how to give an A+ answer to an unhappy customer.

Hi XXXX,

Im sorry for the confusion and your feeling like you got ripped off. The Scout App is new and we are learning a lot about how best to meet peoples different needs. One of the things we learned very early is that the boy using the app has very different needs from the mom/dad using the app, and the scoutmaster is a whole other story. As were gleaning that understanding we are moving towards a new subscription model that will target those needs and only require a single purchase for each of those levels. Hopefully that will make it less confusing. For now we have tried to make it clear in the app description and in app messaging what is required (but we obviously are not doing a good enough job).
 
As for how to solve the current problem, Id like to go ahead and give you a free years subscription to the Scout Pro functionality. If you go to the Misc tab (4th tab) and under premium features, click to sign up with your email, that will backup your data to our databases which will allow me to update your subscription. It is recommended that you sign up anyways so that we have a way of backing up your data in case something happens to your device.
 
As you are a scout master, I wanted to let you know that we are currently in the beta stage of a webpage that allows you to use your computer and your iphone to input data. It is highly useful for scout masters trying to keep track of 10+ scouts. Do you have any interest in getting early access?
 
Thanks,
-Kesler
 
Lets take a second and break it down. Kesler
  1. Apologized right off the bat and acknowledged the negative feelings the customer was experiencing.
  2. Explained our point of view in a non-confrontational way and our plans to make it better in the future.
  3. Fixed the immediate problem. (In this case, the guy feeling ripped off, by giving him a portion of the product for free.)
  4. Went above and beyond and offered the customer something additional that we thought might be useful to them because of their unique position. (This shows that we actually took time to think about this individual.)

When this customer responded, right off the bat, he thanked Kesler for his great customer service. Kesler had turned someone who was about to leave and asking for a refund, back into a happy customer, who is now going to beta-test our new product for us!

Im not saying that its the perfect formula, but you could probably apply some similar principles in dealing with difficult situations and unhappy people you face. People are essentially kind and decent, but we all get frustrated, and its really easy to take your frustration out on people you think are to blame, especially if you feel semi-anonymous. So Ill ask you a favor, be a little nicer to the faceless people in your life that you think cause you problems. Because they are real people with their own issues and frustrations, and theyll probably help you if youre nice to them!
